Praxis · The Toolkit

Advisor Insight
导师锐评

An evidence-driven framework for evaluating academic advisors before you commit years of your career to their group. Powered by PubMed, grant databases, and co-author network analysis — not institutional branding or reputation.

Skill · Hermes Agent · v1.0.0 · by Nigmat Rahim

Why this exists

Every year, thousands of students pick PhD advisors based on reputation, institutional prestige, or a single impressive paper — only to discover years later that the person they chose is not an independent researcher, has no coherent research program, or cannot support their students' careers.

University websites and news articles will always paint the rosiest picture. This tool cuts through that noise by asking a single question: what does the publicly verifiable data say?

The evaluation framework

The analysis proceeds through five sequential phases — each building on the evidence gathered in the previous one. The entire process is data-driven, traceable, and bias-resistant.

0

Disambiguation

Identify the correct person through name+institution+department+ORCID cross-referencing. Never confuse two people with the same name.

1

Publication Audit

Every paper classified by author role (first, last, middle). Independence ratio calculated. Journal quality distribution mapped.

2

Funding Analysis

Grant IDs extracted from PubMed acknowledgments. Each grant attributed to its actual holder — not everyone who listed it.

3

Power Structure

Co-author network reveals who the real boss is, who the peers are, and whether the target has their own group or is a permanent team member.

4

Trajectory

Career timeline reconstructed from publication history. Is this person moving toward or away from independence?

5

Synthesis

7-dimensional scoring (0-10) with weighted overall. Clear bottom line: who this person really is as a researcher.

What we measure

Independence Ratio

The single most informative metric. Calculated as weighted sum of first-author and corresponding-author papers divided by total papers:

Independence = (1.0 × First + 0.7 × CoFirst + 1.0 × LastCorr + 0.5 × CoCorr) / Total
Ratio Meaning
> 0.30Credible independent researcher with own program
0.15 – 0.30Developing independence; transitioning toward PI status
< 0.15Strong team dependence; likely not an independent PI

Truth & bias guardrails

This framework is designed to be maximally objective. Its guardrails are not optional:

✓ Always does
  • Uses only publicly verifiable data
  • States confidence levels explicitly
  • Distinguishes correlation from causation
  • Reports conflicting evidence when sources disagree
  • Prefaces every judgment with supporting data
✗ Never does
  • No personality judgments or character assessments
  • No rumor, hearsay, or third-party claims
  • No institutional prestige bias
  • No "potential" claims — evaluates what exists
  • Never assumes malice or laziness

Disambiguation rule

Chinese names (张伟, 王磊, 刘洋) routinely map to 50+ published researchers. If multiple people share the target's name at different institutions and we cannot confirm the identity with certainty, the analysis stops and presents all candidates with evidence. The user must confirm before proceeding.

Disclosure

All data is from publicly available sources (PubMed, NCBI E-utilities, institutional websites). This analysis is an evidence-based assessment of research metrics, not a personal evaluation. The subject has not been contacted or notified.
